Review: Cobra Kai

In modern times, the demand for remakes of popular movies has come down due to poor reception and general destruction of what made the originals great. The prevalent opinion is that remakes should not exist, but an alternate take or a continuation of the story should be happening. Cobra Kai fulfils the second criteria and is a gateway for modern cinema to the future.

The Karate Kid trilogy, set in the 1980s, was well received and liked by audiences. People liked Daniel LaRusso’s character development and had a genuine connection to him, because of how relatable he was – a high school student who fought his bullies. When the Karate Kid remake, starring Jackie Chan and Jaden Smith, came out in 2010, it was received with less positive reactions. Majority opinion was towards their perception of the original trilogy destroyed. The introduction of Cobra Kai brought about a revolutionary change to movie remakes. Essentially, Cobra Kai is a TV show continuing off from the original Karate Kid trilogy. Since it picks off from that world, the world Daniel LaRusso’s legendary crane kick took place in, people were excited. They would get to see more of their favorite characters without having their experience or viewpoint of the original story jarred.

Cobra Kai is a revolutionary idea because it changes how cinema can be perceived. Instead of having remakes of classic movies, what could happen is a continuation of the story in the form of a TV show. This would save what the original movie stood for and allow creative television series directors to continue stories from the ending of the original movie.
